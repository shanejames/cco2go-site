Segment 2 · The Two Failure Modes

Why Most Health Scores Don't Predict

Failure Mode 1: Vanity Metric Trap
Measuring activity, not outcomes. Login frequency. Tickets opened. QBR attendance.

A customer who logs in daily and does nothing meaningful: not healthy. A customer who logs in 3x/week and built 5 workflows: very healthy.

Frequency tells you almost nothing. Depth tells you almost everything.
Failure Mode 2: Circular Confidence
Score built on what the platform makes easy to collect — not what drives renewal decisions. Usage data is easy. Contract data is available. So the score is built on those.

Champion engagement depth, cross-functional adoption, value realization vs. objectives: harder to instrument. Absent.

Your score is precise but wrong.

The test: last 10 churns at T-90. Were they red or yellow? Yellow = signal quality problem. Not a platform problem. No migration to a new CS tool will fix it.

Segment 3 · The Validated Model

The 6-Signal Validated Health Score

SignalWeightWhat It MeasuresCommon Error
Usage Depth25%Features actively used ÷ relevant features. Breadth + depth of adoption.Most platforms log 'logins' not depth. Activity ≠ value realization.
Adoption Velocity20%Rate of product embedding in customer workflow. Week-over-week active user growth.Teams track totals, not rate of change. Plateau is invisible without velocity tracking.
Support Health15%Volume and severity of unresolved friction. P1/P2 ticket trend.Support data siloed from health score. Biggest data quality gap in most orgs.
Sentiment15%Leading relationship indicator. AI analysis of champion/sponsor communications.Over-weighted due to confirmation bias. CSMs like the metric that confirms their gut.
Financial Stability15%Payment history + contract structure. Involuntary churn predictor.Underused — predicts churn no CSM engagement can prevent. Often completely absent.
Expansion Signals10%Expansion-adjacent feature activation. The only forward-looking signal in the model.Absent from most health scores entirely. Most models are entirely backward-looking.

Weights are starting hypotheses. Validate against actual renewals. Adjust based on evidence, not opinion.

Segment 4 · The Standard

Garbage Score vs. Real Score

Garbage Health Score
Turns green when onboarding completes. Drifts based on CRM fields CSMs fill in manually.

Every CSM has an account perpetually green that churned. And an account perpetually yellow that renewed fine.

Score teaches CSMs to distrust it. So they do. It becomes a compliance field — not a decision-making tool.
Real Health Score
Changes automatically when behavioral signals cross thresholds. CSM doesn't touch it.

Usage Depth drops 30% week-over-week — score moves. Champion communication drops to zero, 21 days — score moves.

CSM receives an alert with a recommended play. Not a dashboard to remember to check.

The test: does your score ever surprise your CSMs? If no — it's not predictive. It's a documentation layer for intuition that already exists.

Segment 5 · The Build Plan

The 3-Phase Build Plan

Most teams try to build Phase 3 on Day 1. What they get is a 6-month implementation nobody trusts because it was never validated.

Phase 1
Days 1–14 · MVP
3 signals only: Usage Depth, Adoption Velocity, Support Health. Equal weights. Top 20 accounts. Manual pull. Spreadsheet is fine.

Goal: find the top 3 risks your current intuition doesn't flag.
Phase 2
Days 15–42 · Validate
Compare Phase 1 scores against actual renewal outcomes. Adjust weights based on evidence. Test: score below 60 at T-90 predicts churn at >70% accuracy?

This step is where most implementations fail by skipping it.
Phase 3
Days 43–90 · Automate
Add remaining 3 signals. Automate via API. Build alert rules on threshold breaches. Weekly score updates. CSM receives alert + recommended play.

You are scaling what you've proven, not automating a guess.

Segment 2 · The 6 Metrics

6 Metrics That Belong
in a Board Deck

Only 2 of these appear in most CS reporting packages. The board language column is what you actually say.

GRR — On renewal slides
"We are not losing the base we've already built."
NRR — Every relevant slide
"Every dollar retained generated X in additional revenue."
CAC vs. Expansion CAC — Quarterly
"Expansion ARR costs 3–5× less than new logo ARR."
Save Play ROI — Quarterly
"Our save play system returned X for every $1 invested."
Most defensible single metric CS can own. Most teams can't calculate it.
T2FV — Every relevant slide
"We deliver value X days faster than benchmark."
Health Score Distribution — Quarterly
"Our at-risk book shrank by X% this quarter."
Segment 3 · What Doesn't Belong

4 Metrics to Retire
from Board Visibility

When you include a metric in a board deck, you make it important. Stop including metrics you don't want boards tracking.

  • NPS
    Near-zero correlation with churn at individual account level. Putting it in the deck implies it matters — boards will benchmark it and ask about it forever.
  • QBRs
    done
    Activity metric. Measures that CS did a thing. Not whether the thing generated value. The equivalent of counting prescriptions without measuring if patients improved.
  • CSM
    ratio
    Staffing metric. Belongs in headcount conversations, not board decks. Says nothing about quality of CS operations or return on those resources.
  • CSAT
    Interaction-level measurement of how a customer felt after one event. Doesn't predict renewal. Doesn't predict churn. Not a CS performance metric.
Segment 4 · The Deck Structure

The 6-Slide Board Deck Formula

1
The One-Sentence Status
NRR this quarter: [X]%. Trend: [up/down] [Y] pts vs. prior. On/off track to [target].
Board reads this and immediately knows if CS is performing. Everything else is supporting evidence.
2
Portfolio Health Map
Green/Yellow/Red distribution + movement vs. prior quarter. Top 3 at-risk with recovery plan.
Proves CS has visibility before problems become visible to the board.
3
NRR Trend (12 Months)
12-month trend line. Label every inflection point — what changed, why it moved.
Trend matters more than point-in-time. Board wants direction, not just current state.
4
T2FV vs. Benchmark
Your T2FV. Industry benchmark. % improvement YoY. Correlation with renewal rate.
Positions CS as value delivery function, not retention function.
5
Save Play ROI
Plays executed. Accounts recovered. ARR at risk. ARR saved. Cost. Return = [$].
The most defensible revenue impact statement CS can put in front of a board.
6
The Ask
Specific dollar or headcount. Expected NRR impact. Timeline. One slide.
"We'd like to add headcount" gets tabled. A specific ask with projected 3.0× return gets funded.
Segment 5 · Language Translation

CS Language vs.
Revenue Language

Same metrics. Completely different conversation. The framing determines how the board receives everything that follows.

CS Language — Gets Nodded At
"We improved our customer health scores and expanded our save play coverage to 87% of at-risk accounts."
"We reduced average time to first value by 8 days this quarter."
Revenue Language — Gets Follow-Up Questions
"We prevented an estimated $2.3M in ARR from churning. At our CAC of $41K per new logo, that is the equivalent of retaining 56 new customer acquisitions — without the acquisition cost."
"The cohort onboarded this quarter is projected to renew 4 points higher than last year's. At current ARR, that's approximately $180K in additional retained revenue."
