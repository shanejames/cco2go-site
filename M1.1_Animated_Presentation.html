Why the Inventor Is Frustrated
When a Measure
Becomes a Target
Goodhart's Law: When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.
Fred Reichheld designed NPS as a simple directional indicator. What it became was a target that companies optimized for — cherry-picking who gets surveyed, timing surveys after positive interactions, coaching customers to give higher scores.

Forrester's CX Index: Consumer perceptions of CX quality hit an all-time low — while NPS scores held steady or went up. The metric says things are improving. The customers say otherwise.

The 4 Structural Failures — 1 of 4
Failure 01:
Doesn't Predict Retention
!
A 9/10 customer can still churn
The survey respondent liked the product — but wasn't the economic buyer. Or they gave a nine six months ago and since then their usage dropped to zero. NPS measures stated intent at a single point in time.
!
Retention is behavioral, not attitudinal
Retention is a behavioral outcome over a long period. Treating a moment-in-time sentiment survey as a predictor of behavior over 12 months is a fundamental category error.

Failure 02:
Treats All Customers Equally
$300K
Enterprise detractor ARR at risk. One enterprise detractor can cost you hundreds of thousands in ARR and create a competitor reference.
≈ $0
Freemium promoter referral impact. A small-account promoter contributes almost nothing to your referral pipeline — yet counts identically in your NPS score.
By collapsing everything into one number, NPS obscures the signals that actually matter — the accounts that will define your next renewal cycle.

The 4 Structural Failures — 3 of 4
Failure 03:
Creates False Confidence
NPS is 70. Board is happy. Team is happy. Nobody is actively looking for problems.

Then three of your top-20 accounts don't renew. And everyone is blindsided.
The warning signs were there. In the usage data. In the support ticket velocity. In the declining engagement patterns. They just weren't in the NPS score, because NPS doesn't look at those things.

False confidence is more dangerous than no confidence. At least if you knew you had a problem, you'd look for it.

The 4 Structural Failures — 4 of 4
Failure 04:
Invites Manipulation
REAL EXAMPLE
"I once worked with a company where CS team bonuses were tied to NPS. Within two quarters, they had perfected survey timing — send right after a successful implementation, never after a support escalation, send to the champion not the frustrated end user."
The score went up. The customer experience did not change by a single percent.
When your measurement is gameable and you tie compensation to it, you get exactly what you incentivize — a better score. And nothing you actually want.

The Most Important Concept in Health Scoring
Trajectory >
Snapshot
HIGH RISK
70% Health · Declining
Was 85% three months ago. Still looks "okay" in a snapshot. Heading toward a cliff. Intervention needed now — not at next QBR.
IMPROVING
50% Health · Rising
Was 35% three months ago. Still below median but heading in the right direction. Watch and reinforce — this account is finding its footing.
NPS cannot capture this. A usage snapshot cannot capture this. Engagement velocity can — and it catches churn signals weeks before any other method.

Before You Watch Module 1.2
Do This Monday
1
Calculate your actual NPS response rate for the last 12 months. If it's below 10%, you are making decisions on statistically meaningless data. Write the number down.
2
Pick your 5 highest-NPS and 5 lowest-NPS accounts. For each one, look at actual usage data, support ticket history, and engagement pattern. Write down the discrepancies between NPS and reality.
3
Build a simple health score for your top 20 accounts using 3 data points you already have: login frequency, support ticket velocity, engagement trend. Crude but more predictive than NPS on day one.
